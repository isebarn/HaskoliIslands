Project Management

Project management delivers tools to organizations that need to improve their

planning, implementation and management of the activities they need to

complete

Project management was onece a "special need" management.

However, more recently they have become the standard way of doing business

There are simple reasons why project management has become as important as

it is

1. Compression of the product life cycle

As the driving force of competition has exploded, especially in the tech

industrym companies must develop products and produce them in

a exponentially faster time frame. 30 years ago, a product could have

a 15 year lifecycle. In today's world, the average life cycle is 1-3 years

With that in mind, it is obvious that indtroducing a product to the market

as soon as possible greatly increases its time present on the market

2. Knowledge explosion

Things all around us are rapidly becoming more complex. 30 years ago

laying a road was as simple as calculating necessary raw materials, hiring

people and laying the road. Today, immense calculations and planning

are required that complicate things greatly, but also provide a better, longer

lasting road.

If "simple" things like laying a road have become more complex, it is easy to

reason that complex things like cellphones, automobiles, televisions etc are

monumentaly more complex than 30 years ago

3. Triple bottom line (planet, people, profit)

In recent years, and especially in recent years, enviromental factors have risen

enormously in importance. A company cannot simply do anything to maximize its

profits at the expense of the atmosphere, ocean, land or living things

4. Corporate downsizing

Restructuring of the typical organization has had a huge effect on middle management

Middle management has effectively been replaced by project management, as middle

management by its design is not remotely as efficient as a dedicated project manager

5. Increased customer focus

As competition in every market has increased, customer satisfaction has become a

leading priority. Customers want customized products and it is the project managers

responsibility that the projects is steered in the direction the customer wants

Project

A project is a temporary endeavour to create a unique product/service/result

A project is different from a program in the sence that a program is a group of related projects

designed to accomplish a common goal

A project also has "two dimesions" that need to be intertwined. Sucessfully implementing a project

is not simply about gathering people that have the tecnical know-how to complete the project, but

there is a sociocultural side aswell

The sociocultural side refers to skills regarding leadership, problem solving, teamwork abilities,

negotations, politics and satisfying customer expectations

Meanwhile, the technological side that everyone is more of less familiar with, dels with the

scope of the project, schedules, allocating resources, tracking costs and status reports and

breaking the project down into executable work packages

Projects are integrated through the portfolio management. The portfolio management

-Oversees project selection

-Monitors resource levels and skills

-Encourages use of best practices

-Balances projects in the portfolio to represent a risk level that is ideal for the organization

-Improves communication among all stakeholders

-Creates a total organization perspective

-Improves the management of projects

 Strategic management

Strategic management is assessing what an organization wants to be and deciding and

implementing how to get there

The important factors of strategic management are:

1. Responding to changes in the external enviroment

2. Allocating the scarce resources of the firm to improve its competitive position

3. Responding to new action programs that enhance the competitive position of the firm

With strategic management we can position the organization to ease its goal, which

is to satisfy customers

With a long term position identified, objectives are decided and stretegies designed

to accomplish them

There are four activities involved in strategic management

1. Review and define the organization mission

The mission is to decide what we want to be

A mission statement identifies the scope of the organization in terms of its product

Such a statement provides a focus for making decisions, such as wheter to accept

a project or not

2. Set long-range goals and objectives

Objectives translate the mission into specific terms. Furthermore they describe

how we get there

3. Analyze and formulate strategies to reach objectives

Formulating a strategy describes "what" needs to be done to reach an objective

There are five concepts to keep in mind when formulating a strategy.

-Being specific when targeting an objective

-Create a measurement to determine progress along the way

-Make the objective assigneable to a single person

-State what could be accomplished with available resources

-State when the objective could be completed

4. Implement strategies through projects

Implementing answers how strategies will be realized given some resources

 Functional organization

The simplest way to organizing a project, is to simply manage it using the existing hiearchy

in the organization. Coordination is maintained with the usual management channel

The advantages of the functional organization as a means to manage a project are:

1. No change to the organizational structure, as it is used to manage the project

2. Flexibility in staff, as employees are easily assigned to isolated tasks and then returned

3. In-depth expertise for projects that have a narrow scope

4. Easy post project transition when project completes

Its drawbacks are less appealing

1. Lack of focus. This is due to the fact that employees arent considered a part of a project

and they might be brought in for a single task. This results in them not commiting

to the task at hand

2. Poor integration. There is a chance of poor integration across functional unit groups, as

each group feels their project is the most crucial one and give little concern for other

parts of the total project

3. Slow. It generally takes longer for functional groups to complete projects. This is due to

slow response time, as information needs to circulate through normal management

4. Lack of ownership. The project may be seen as an additional burden on employees that are

assigned to some additional tasks

 Dedicated teams

Dedicated team is an organizational structure where all of the resources necessary for the project

are assigned full time

Dedicated teams operate as separate units from the rest of the organization. A full time manager

is assigned and his first job is to pick a team from specialist who will also be full time on the

project, both from within and outside the organization

The parent organization has various ways to keep a leash on the team, usually a financial constrain

Other firms are built to support projects, where most work within the organization is accomplished

with dedicated teams. These organizations are known as "Projectized Organizations"

The benefits of the dedicated team structure are:

1. Simple. As human resources are taken from a pool an assigned full time, team members

wont need to be scheduled time with the team and with the rest of the functional

organization

2. Fast. These projects usually get done quickly as team members are both dedicated to the

project and thus focused, and additionally cherry picked from a pool, so they are

expected to be more or less qualified for their tasks.

In addition, information runs quickly accross the team every time a decision needs

to be made, guaranteeing that it gets made quickly

3. Cohesiveness. As everyone is dedicated to the projects, cohesion forms amongst team

members and they strive to do the best job they can

4. Cross-functional integration. As each member is picked especially for a job, not only can

specialists accomplish some task faster, but better

There are some concerns on these teams. Although these do not affect the project itself but rather

the organization, they need to be addressed regardless

1. Expensive. These projects are way more expensive as personell are assigned full time but

might not be doing actual work full time. This is due to the fact that person A might be

waiting for person B to complete task X, so that he can begin work on task Y. In the meantime

he does not go to the parent organization to assist on other tasks, he does nothing of value

2. Internal strife. The team may feel like they are an autonomous entity. Not a huge problem

but a problem nonetheless as it undermines the parent organization to have a team that

has begun to regard itself as "better than the rest of the company"

3. Limited technological expertise. Even though team members are chosen with expertise in

mind when the team is formed, in some areas their expertise may be limited

4. Difficult post-project transition. When the project completes, the team members may find it

difficult to re-integrate into the parent organization

 Matrix arrangement

The matrix arrangement has a project management structure "laid" onto the normal functional

hierarchy and includes "two" chains of command. The functional command and the project

command, both of which project participants report to

The matrix structure is designed to optimize resources by having individuals working on

multiple projects as well as performing on normal, functional duties

There are 3 types of matrices

1. Weak matrix. This is similar to the functional approach, except a project manager

is assigned to coordinating project activities.

Functional managers are responsible for their segment of a project and "call the shots"

Project managers are responsible for creating schedules, collecting info and project status

A weak matrix is suited for projects where technical quality is necessary

2. Balanced matrix. Here, the project manager is responsible in defining what needs to be

done and defining it, while the functional manager is concerned in how it will be accomplished

The project manager creates a plan, sets schedules and monitors progress

The functinoal manager assigns personell and executes their segment of the project

A balanced matrix balances project requirements with technical requirements

3. Strong matrix. This structure creates the illusion of a project team within the matrix enviroment

The project manager controls most aspects of the project. Who does what, what will be done.

The functional manager has title over his people and is somewhat regarded as a subcontractor

A strong matrix works best where project integration is important as it improves control

over project activities and costs

The benefits of a matrix organization are

1. Efficiency. Resources may be shared accross projects as well as within functional divisions

2. Strong project focus, as there is a formal project manager responsible for coordinating

3. Easy post-project transition, as individuals are tied up in two or more projects, completing

a project does not require them to integrate themselves into project halfway complete

4. Flexible. Resources are shared more flexibly so expertise across the firm can be utilized better

There are some weaknesses though

1. Dysfunctional conflict. Tension between project and functional managers can have a

huge negative effect

2. Infighting. When resources are being shared across projects, some infighting can occur

between teams

3. Stressful. Every team member has two supervisors

4. Slow

  Balanced matrix

A balanced matrix has the project manager and the functional manager sharing equal authority

over the project

The project manager decides what needs to be done

The functional manager decides how it will be done

  Strong Matrix

estimates

Estimates serve as to

-Support good decisions

-Schedule work

-Determine how long and how much project should cost

-Determine if a project is worth doing

-Develop cash flow needs

-Show how well a project is progressing

-Develop time phased budgets

Projects are estimated either top-down or bottom-up

Top-down estimations are made by someone with experience in top management, who

might not have that much knowledge of the project itself

Bottom up estimates are preffered if projects are sufficiently small, as they will be accurate

either way as there isnt that much uncertainty in small projects anyway

Conradictory, bottom up estimates are also used for high uncertainty projects becouse the

uncertainty cannot be harmonized between work pagages

If the scope is not adequetly defined, top managers will need to make the estimates as there

isnt anything solid enough for project managers or staff to estimate accurately

Bottom-up estimates are preffered as they offer much more accurate estimates, and ofcourse

offer more confidence in their estimates

If projects are time/cost important, they are estimated on the work package level, consequently

if the contract has a fixed price, the estimates are made at a work package level

Lastly, if the customer is expected to be involved in decisions estimates are obviously made

on the work package level

 Quality of estimations

Estimates are considered to be good if they are 95% accurate

Factors that influence the quality of estimates are

1. Planning horizon.

Meaning time. Estimates made for tomorrow will be fairly accurate while

estimates for 3 months from now may not be accurate at all

2. Project duration.

Related to the planning horizon. The longer a project is, more uncertainty is

introduced in the planning horizon

3. People

People tend to pad their estimates to ensure they are on time. They are also sometimes

asked to estimate another person which will obviously introduce erros

4. Project structure and organization.

One of the advantages of a dedicated project team, is that there is confidence in the

estimates in the team, as everyone knows exactly what needs to be done, and they

have all the time they need

This quality is not inherited in matrix structure

5. Padding estimates

Employees estimating their own work arent the only ones that pad estimates, project

managers also pad the combined estimates from the team. In addition, they pad the

cost estimates so they prove to be under budget when the project completes

6. Organizational culture

Some organizations accept padding of estimates, which in turn encourages the padding

of project managers and individual workers. Other organizations do not and might

strongly discourage it

The ones responsible for work, i.e persons at the work package level, should do their own

estimates as they are most familiar with it.

Using several people if possible as a consensus of many experts will improve estimates even

further

Estimates should also be produced considering normal conditions, not optimal or worst case

scenarios, but using what seems to be the average on a normal day

Time units should be created that are universal over the project. A single time unit might be 3 days as

it may not make sence to use single days as a measuring device as it is to precise

Lastly, estimates should not assume that contingency plans are in place

 Cost estimates

There are a few ways to make cost estimations, depending if estimating is made top-down

or bottom up

Top-down methods

1. Consensus method

Estimates made by top managers that pool together their experience on past

projects that feel similar

2. Ratio methods

Related to the consensus method, but more scientific. Similar projects are

weighed by similarity and cost, and a concrete number calculated. Fx if

a past project was laying 100m of pipe, and the project under discussion is

laying 500 meters of pipe, the cost will be slightly less than 5-times that of the

former project

3. Apportion methods

An extention of the ratio method. If a project is close enough to a past project, that

projects data is used

4. Learning curves

When a project is composed of doing the same thing many times, there will be a learning

curve. Each time the quantity doubles, unit hours are reduced at a constant rate

Managers realise this and see that if a project will be building 50 identical houses, the first

3 houses will be slow, houses 4-6 half as quick and so forth

Bottom-up methods

1. Template methods

If a project is similar to a project someone has already done, they can expect that individual

to be able to complete the new task in at least the same ammount of time

2. Parametric procedures applied to specific tasks

Like the ratio method

3. Range estimating

If a project has high uncertainty, there will be given a range, low, medium and high

  Apportionment

The apportaion method has all costs allocated to a specific segment of a project using a percentage

of planned total cost. fx framing a house might use 25% of the total cost

  Function points

Function points are derived from past software projects to estimate current project time and cost

  Delphi method

  Learning curves

The learning curve is a mathematical curve used to predict how a repeated tasks duration

  Ratio methods

Ratio methods use past actual projects that are noticably similar

  Phase estimating

Phase estimating is an iterative estimation process, where in the begining there is a macro estimate for

  Template method

The template method is a prepared form to develop project networks, costs and time estimates

Allocating resources on time constrained projects and resource constrained projects requires knowledge

on two different methods

   Time constrained

Time constrained projects: Resource smoothing

When projects are time constrained, the best strategy is maximizing resource utilizating

to make sure all resources are as productive as possible. This is called resource smoothing

If there are three tasks, A, B and C, needing 1,2 and 1 resource respectively, and we have

2 resources, we want to work on A and C in parallel and then work on B, instead of first

working on A, then B, then C

To level resources we take our project network and observe which activities have slack and

those activities may be pushed back. If fx task A,B and C take 1,2 and 1 weeks respectively and

project D is dependant on the completion of all of them, we work 2 weeks on B and then 1 week

on A and C, or vice versla

There is a drawback to leveling, as it eats up our slack and indtroduces the risk of a new critical

path, and when a critical path is delayed, the whole project is delayed, which ironically is exactly

what we're trying to prevent when leveling

Resource leveling accomplishes both reducing the peak demand for resources, shortens the

requirement for resources over the span of the project, in addition to minimize fluctuations

in resource demand.

Minimizing fluctuations has more benefit on some projects, if a resource is in one place and

needs to be moved, that takes time, how much time is dependant on the resource, if

its a programmer, he can easily drive to a new place, but if its a a chemical silo containing

ammonia, that will be a quick project all by itself to move

   Resource constrained

When resources arent adequate to meet peak demant requirements, there is a resouce problem

The project network is a good tool to remedy this constraint. There exists a method called the

parallel method that delays activities by choosing the best one to delay

If there are 3 tasks, A,B and C that should all run in paralell, but cant becouse of resource

constraints, we delay one of them or more according to the following pecking order

1. Minimum slack

2. Smallest duration

3. Identification number

Meaning if A,B and C have slack 2, but B slack of 0, we delay either A or C.

If then A takes 1 week to complete but C takes 2 weeks, we delay A

If A and C would both take 1 week to complete, we would delay either one, usually simply

by identification number, so C

  Pros and Cons

The reasons to outsource are numerous

1. Cost reduction

When a task or tasks can be outsourced offshore to countries like China or Ingia, cost

will drop significantly, and as a bonus, overhead costs will drop aswell

2. Faster project completion

If outsourcing is a viable option, i.e more cheaply, it means that the project may

be completed earlier than expected, as a work package or packages arent

the organizations concern untill they are complete

3. High level of expertise

If a task needs huge technological knowhow, and the organization isnt staffed for

such a task, outsourcing to a qualified contractor results in higher quality in less time

4. Flexibility

Outsourcing makes an organization less dependant on their own resources

The drawbacks are just as many

1. Coordination breakdowns

There is a specific challenge in managing project over different organizations. Needless

to say if a task in outsourced to China or India or another foreign country, there can

be language barriers, cultural differences etc

2. Loss of control

If many tasks are outsourced to many different contractors, all a part of the same project

there will be huge difficulties if just a signle one fails to deliver

3. Conflict

Projects are prone to conflicts if the participants do not belong to the same organization

4. Security issues

Depending on the nature of the project, trade or business secrets may be revealed

 Negotiating

Negotiating should not be the art of getting as much as you can for as little as possible

Negotiating should not be a contest, it should be a way for everyone to win

1. Separate people from the problem

Focus on what needs to be done not on the people that will do it. Emotions should not

run a negotiation.

2. Focus on interests, not positions

If you need something for 50$, but the contractor says it will cost 100$, do not hold on

to your pride.

Negotiate what is the fair ammount. Make the project his project so this isnt just a pot

of gold for him

3. Invent options for mutual gain

If the contractor feels like he is a part of the project and he has something to gain, negotiating

will go smoother

4. Use objective criteria

5. Dealing with unreasonable people

BATNA (best alternative to a negotiated agreement)

If the person you have to negotiate with is unreasonable, you just walk away untill that person

understands that you are looking for a win/win scenario and nothing less

If a vendor wants 100$ for something that you urgently need, but 80$ would be the fair

price, just accept, and immedietally start looking for other suppliers

  BATNA

BATNA or Best Alternative To A Negotiated Agreement

BATNA indicates your power to negotiate with the other party

If you have weak BATNA and the one you negotiate with is giving you no option, you simply must

meet his demands. However afterwards you should try to increase your BATNA with that

individual
